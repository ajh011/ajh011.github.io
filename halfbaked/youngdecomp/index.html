Young Decompostions of Tensors and GL-Invariant Spaces.

Alex Heilman
May 1, 2025
Mathematics of Physics

Young diagrams and their corresponding standard tableux allow for the definiton of symmetrizers, operators which act on elements of tensor spaces and return projections onto invariant subspace over the General Linear group on the tensor space.

An invariant subspace with respect to some group \(G\) is one which is closed under operations \(g\in G\). More intuitively, 'invariant' just means the operations in \(G\) don't take any element in the space outside of itself. Naturally then, one may wonder about the smallest possible invariant subspaces of arbitrary tensor spaces, referred to as irreducible subspaces.

Here we seek an approach to the decomposition of tensors (taken generally as \(\mathbb{C}^n\)) with respect to their corresponding General Linear groups \(GL(\mathbb{C}^n)\). That is, we present the mathematics neccesary to construct projections onto irreducible invariant subspaces of tensors with respect to all invertible linear transformations on them.

Identifying and categorizing these irreducible spaces is essentially an application of group representation theory. The representations of \(GL(\mathbb{C}^n)\) are, by way of the Schur-Weyl Duality, indexed exactly by the symmetric group \(S_n\) acting on the indices of the tensor space with operations \(\sigma_i\in S_n\).

The irreducible representations of \(S_n\) may be described diagramatically by Young tableaux, constructed from Young diagrams of order \(n\) and an ordered set of tensor indices \({x_1, x_2, ..., x_n}\). These tableaux each correpsond to a Young symmetrizer, which act on tensors and return irreducible invariant (under both \(GL\) and \(S\)) subtensors.

Below, we first complete the motivation of this problem with a simple, likely familiar example. We then provide the tools to generalize the concept of \(GL\) invariant subspaces of tensor spaces, namely: Young diagrams, tableaux, and most importantly symmetrizers.

Example: Symmetric and Anti-symmetric Matrices

Conisder a tensor of order 2, represented by a matrix \(M\) with components \(M_{ij}\). An arbitrary matrix may always be broken into it's totally symmetric components \(S_{ij}\) and anti-symmetric components \(A_{ij}\), defined as:

\begin{align*} S_{ij} &= \frac{1}{2}(M_{ij}+M_{ji})\\ A_{ij} &=\frac{1}{2}(M_{ij}-M_{ji})\\ \Rightarrow M_{ij}&=S_{ij}+A_{ij} \end{align*}

Conclusion & Extensions

Further decompositons with respect to subgroups of \(GL\) often can give more insight into invariants in specific applications, such as the orthogonal groups, dihedral groups, cyclic groups, etc.
