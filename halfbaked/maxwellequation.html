Maxwell's Equation in Geomteric Form.

Alex Heilman
December 23, 2021
Mathematics of Physics

Maxwell's equations are known to most all physicists as a set of four differential (or integral) equations. However, with different mathematical frameworks, we may reduce these equations to smaller sets, albeit with potentially greater notational baggage. But somewhat recent developments in frameworks give a rather concise and conceptaually clean unification of the four into one equation.

Maxwell's equations along with the Lorentz force law, in principle, characterize all of classical electrodynamics. Arguably, they're the nicest force for classical physicists to deal with, given it all comes down to "simple" vector calculus. In most textbooks on the subject, and likely in the minds of most physicists, they're generally stated as the four differential equations, and often they'll be restated in integral form (which is still in the vector framework). We'll first look at a few different forms of the equations in different mathematical frameworks, and then exhibit the shiny new equation of geometric algebra (along with some context).

Common Form of the Equations

In the course of most introductory electrodynamics courses, Maxwell's equations are generally given as a set of four equations in the Heaviside-Gibbs vector formalism. These can be described either in differential or integral form (note here we're not talking about differential forms, just a differential equation as opposed to an integral equation). These forms of the equations are given below.

Maxwell's Equations in Vector Formalism

Differential Equations
$$ \nabla\cdot \mathbf{E}=\frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} $$ $$ \nabla\times \mathbf{E}=-\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} $$ $$ \nabla\cdot \mathbf{B}=0 $$ $$ \nabla\times \mathbf{B}=\frac{\mathbf{J}}{c^2\epsilon_0}+\frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} $$
Integral Equations
$$ \oint\mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{A}=\frac{q}{\epsilon_0} $$ $$ \oint\mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{s}=-\frac{d\Phi_B}{d t} $$ $$ \oint\mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{A}=0 $$ $$ \oint\mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{s}=\mu_0 i+\frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \int\mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{A} $$

More sophisticated treatments include the tensor form of the equations and a recasting as a set of equations of differential forms. Special relativity is often included in these more intensive formalisms.

Another distinction to make here is that we're considering the microscopic case of the equations, in that we expect to be in free space, and hence only consider the permitivity and permeability of free space.
