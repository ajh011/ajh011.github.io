Symplectic & Variational Mechanics

Mechanics, or the evolution of dynamical systems, is reduced to a problem of the calculus of variations via Lagrangian and Hamiltonian mechanics. These frameworks for mechanics presuppose that the natural evolution of dynamical systems is somehow 'stationary' for variations in path. That is, the path taken by an object in physical and phase space is the minimization of some integral, called the action.

The most modern mathematical framework of mechanics defines the space of configurations as a \(2n\)-dimensional manifold with some symplectic form (a non-degenerate 2-form) that defines a 'flow' for all points in the configuration space. This symplectic form is akin to the Hamiltonian of the usual variational mechanics and generates the dynamical behaviour of the system via the associated flow.

Variational Mechanics

The form of the classical Lagrangian, \( L=T-V \), can be derived from the assumption of D'Alembert's principle, an adaption of the principle of virtual work to dynamical systems. Hamilton's principle itself can also be taken as the starting point from which static and instantenous systems can be derived. Below, all three principles are described.

The Principle of Virtual Work

The principle of virtual work is a mathematical framework for systems of static equilibrium. It essentially states that the total virtual work of the system is equal to zero for static systems:

$$ \text{Principle of Virtual Work:} \quad \sum_i F_i^{total} \cdot \delta \vec{r}=0 $$

D'Alembert's Principle

The principle of virtual work can be adapted to instants of dynamical systems by treating reactions of objects as inertial or 'reactive' forces via the Newtonian relation \( \frac{dp}{dt}=I\). Mathematically, this turns the statment into (assuming harmonious motions):

$$ \text{D'Alembert's Principle:} \quad \sum_i \left(F_i^a- I_i \right) \cdot \delta \vec{r}=0. $$

Hamilton's Principle

For dynamical systems with some path through time, as opposed to reactions in instants, Hamilton's principle can be applied. Mathematically, it has the form:

$$ \text{Hamilton's Principle:} \quad \delta\int_{t_a}^{t_b} L(q,\dot{q})\ dt=0. $$
Where here it is presupposed that there is some functional fully describing the evolution of the system. This functional is the action, \(s=\int L \ dt\), and thus we have a true problem amenable to the calculus of variations.
