Classical Electrodynamics

Basic Relations

The electric field is a non-rotational vector field that is postulated to permeate all space. The field uniques determines the force on a charge particle via the relation

$$ F=qE $$
The electric field (because of it's vanishing curl) may be specified as the gradient of some scalar potential
$$ E=-\nabla \Phi $$

Electrostatic Potential in Terms of Green's Functions

The most general procedure for calculating electrostatic potentials, given some boundary conditions, is achieved through the use of Green's functions, which are effectively integral operators that invert the differntial operator through which boundary conditions are specified.

\begin{align} \Phi(\vec{x})=\frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0}\int_V \rho (\vec{x}') &G(\vec{x},\vec{x}')d\vec{x}' \ \\ &+\oint\left[ \frac{\Phi(\vec{x}')}{\partial \hat{n}}G(\vec{x},\vec{x}') -\Phi(\vec{x}') \frac{\partial G(\vec{x},\vec{x}')}{\partial \hat{n}}\right]da'\\ \end{align}

Types of Boundary Conditions

Boundary conditions are generally given as either one of two classes: Dirichlet or von Neumann. Both specify a certain equivalence along some boundary, and have corresponding behaviour neccesarily exhibited in their corresponding Green's functions.

Dirichlet

Dirichlet boundary condtions specify the potential along the boundary and hence specify \( \Phi\). Under these conditions, the Green's function along the boundary surface neccesarily vanished, that is \(G\vert_S=0\)

von Neumann

Neumann boundary condtions specify the electric field along the boundary and hence specify \(\frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial \hat{n}}\). Under these conditions, the Green's function derivative along the boundary surface neccesarily vanished, that is \(\frac{\partial G}{\partial \hat{n}}\vert_S=0\)
