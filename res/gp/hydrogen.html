The Hydrogen Atom

One of the few solvable systems in quantum mechanics just so happens to be the non-relativistic model of the Hydrogen atom. That is, we may describe the quantum mechanical system due to the Coulombic interaction between a small particle and another taken to be immovable.

Non-relativistic Hamiltonian

We take the basic interaction between the electron and proton to be Coulombic attraction, and model the proton as immovable (given its large mass compared to the electron on the order of \(10^4\)). In this approach we then have only two terms in the Hamiltonian defining this model: one term accounting for the three dimensional momentum of the electron; and another accounting for this Coulombic force between the electron and the proton. The Hamiltonian is then given explicitly as:

$$ \hat{H}=-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\nabla^2+\frac{-e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{1}{r} $$
This, as stated in the introduction, has a nice closed form solution. This analytic solution may be given by a technique often used to solve Helmholtz-like differential equations, which is discussed briefly below.

Approach

The technique most often used to solve the differential equation for our model of the Hydrogen atom is that of separation of variables. Given the above Hamiltonian, we have the corresponding differential equation given by the time-independent Schrodinger equation:

\begin{align} \hat{H}\psi(r,\theta,\phi)-E\psi(r,\theta,\phi)&=0\\ \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\nabla^2+\frac{-e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{1}{r}-E\right]\psi(r,\theta,\phi)&=0 \end{align}

We now apply separation of variables, in which we 'separate' the dependence of the function of three variables into three functions depending on one variable, and assume our final solution (that dependent on three variables) to be a product of these independently dependent functions. Explicity, we write:

\begin{align} \psi(r,\theta,\phi)&=R(r)\Theta(\theta)\Phi(\phi) \end{align}
which we insert into our differential equation and divide through by the above form of \(\psi\), yielding three differential separated equations for each independent spherical variable (don't try this in Cartesian coordinates, due to the symmetry in the Hamiltonian).

\begin{align} \frac{d}{dr}\left(r^2\frac{dR}{dr}\right) +\frac{2mr^2}{\hbar^2}\left(E+\frac{e^2}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r}\right)R-\alpha R&=0\\ \frac{\sin\theta}{\Theta}\frac{d}{d\theta}\left(\sin\theta\frac{d\Theta}{d\theta}\right)+\alpha\sin^2\theta-\beta&=0\\ \frac{1}{\Phi^2}\frac{d^2\Phi}{d\phi^2}+\beta=0 \end{align}
The solutions of these differential equations are described below.

Energy Levels

The energy levels of the non-relativistic Hydrogen Hamiltonian can be shown to only depend on the principal quantum number \(n\). These energy levels may be given by the formula:

\begin{align} E_n=-\left[\frac{m}{2\hbar^2}\left(\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\right)^2\right]\frac{1}{n^2} \end{align}
where \(m\) is the mass of an electron at rest, \(e\) is the charge of the electron, and \(\epsilon_0\) the permitivity of free space.

Wavefunctions (Eigenfunctions of \(H\))

The total solution of the Hamiltonian defined above gives position space wavefunctions described by three indexes of the form:

\begin{align} \psi_{nlm}(r,\theta,\phi)=\sqrt{\left(\frac{m_e e^2}{2\pi n \epsilon_0\hbar^2}\right)^3\frac{(n-l-1)!}{2n(n+l)!}}e^{-\frac{m_e e^2}{2\pi n \epsilon_0\hbar^2}r}\left(\frac{m_e e^2}{4\pi n \epsilon_0\hbar^2}r\right)^lL^{2l+1}_{n-l-1}\left(\frac{m_e e^2}{4\pi n \epsilon_0\hbar^2}r\right)Y_l^m(\theta,\phi) \end{align}
Which are normalized over integrals of all space and which, over such intervals, are orthogonal for \(n,l,m\neq n',l',m'.\)
