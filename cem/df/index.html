Differential Forms

Differential forms are what you're actually integrating everytime you use an integral (i.e. every integrand you've ever written). They're geometrically interpretable as oriented areas, of a dimension equal to the order of the form. Equivalently, they're the restriction on the tensor product space of some vector space to only antisymmetric components. Given some function \( f \) defined in some coordinate space \( x_n \) the exterior derivative can be taken as below:

$$ df(x_1,x_2,...x_n)=\sum_{n}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\wedge dx_n $$

Three Dimensions

In three dimensions, the exterior derivative's relevance to physics is easily seen. In conjunction with the relevant Hodge Star operator (here the unique 3-form \( dx\wedge dy\wedge dz \)), the gradient, the curl, the divergence, and the Laplacian are all aptly generalized by the exterior derivative.

1-forms

In three dimensions, the gradient is generalized exactly by the exterior derivative. Gradients are effectively all exact 1-forms, but instead of unit vectors being associated with the relevant partial derivative, unit 1-forms are associated with the relevant partial derivatives.

$$ df(x,y,z) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}d\hat{x}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}d\hat{y}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}d\hat{z} $$
